# Yuri Alves Portfolio Content Guide

## Purpose

This portfolio should do three jobs at the same time:

1. Show what you have built and shipped.
2. Act as a running log of what you are learning, testing, and thinking about.
3. Represent who you are as a builder, not just what tools you know.

The site should feel like a live operating surface, not a static online CV.

## Core Positioning

The strongest positioning for you is:

**Builder across software, automation, and hardware.**

More specifically:

- You build practical products fast.
- You care about real-world usefulness more than over-engineering.
- You move between code, systems, product thinking, and experimentation.
- You are comfortable shipping polished work and weird experiments in the same public identity.

That is what the content should reinforce over and over again.

## What Visitors Should Understand Fast

Within the first 20 to 30 seconds, someone landing on the site should understand:

- who you are
- what kind of work you do
- what makes your approach different
- what you are building now
- where to go next if they want proof

The homepage should answer:

- Why should I care about Yuri?
- What has he actually built?
- What is he focused on right now?
- Is he thoughtful or just technical?
- What kind of person is behind the work?

## Audience

Your content should work for four audiences:

### Founders and clients

They want to know:

- can you build fast
- can you think beyond implementation
- can you solve ugly operational problems
- can you ship without hand-holding

### Employers or collaborators

They want to know:

- how broad your skillset is
- whether you can own work end to end
- whether you can work across product, frontend, backend, and systems
- whether you write and think clearly

### Other builders

They want:

- useful technical breakdowns
- lessons from real projects
- practical opinions
- build logs and experiments

### People discovering you personally

They want a sense of:

- your personality
- your values
- your taste
- your long-term direction

## Content Principles

Everything on the site should follow these rules:

- show proof, not just claims
- speak from experience, not generic advice
- write like a builder, not like a marketer
- keep it direct and clear
- be opinionated without pretending to know everything
- make your work feel alive and current

Good content for your site sounds like:

- "Here is what I built."
- "Here is why it mattered."
- "Here is what went wrong."
- "Here is what I learned."
- "Here is what I am trying next."

Bad content for your site sounds like:

- vague motivational founder language
- generic “I am passionate about technology” copy
- inflated startup buzzwords
- stack lists with no context
- portfolio descriptions that say what the app is but not why it matters

## The Narrative Structure Of The Site

Your portfolio should feel like this:

1. **Poster**
   Visitors immediately get your identity and current focus.
2. **Proof**
   Featured projects and work history show you actually ship.
3. **Thinking**
   Posts and build logs show your process and judgment.
4. **Person**
   The about section explains how you think and what drives you.
5. **Connection**
   The final section makes it easy to reach you or follow your work.

## What Content Should Live On The Homepage

The homepage should not try to say everything. It should create a strong impression and route people to the right proof.

### Hero

The hero should communicate:

- your identity as a builder
- your working style
- your current focus
- one reason to trust you

Recommended content:

- a hard headline with energy and specificity
- one short supporting paragraph
- current role or current mode
- focus tags
- one personal mission statement
- 2 calls to action

The hero should not be a résumé summary. It should be your opening statement.

### Featured Projects

This should show your three strongest or most strategic bets.

For you, these should usually be:

- Legocia
- the automation-first agency model
- one or two practical product experiments that show speed and range

Each featured project should answer:

- what it is
- why it exists
- what makes it interesting
- what kind of builder it proves you are

### Work

The work section should prove consistency.

It should show:

- you have worked in real environments
- you can deliver in startups and larger organizations
- you have held responsibility
- your path makes sense

Do not let this section become a dry list of job titles. Each role needs one useful summary line that says what you actually did or what it taught you.

### Build Logs / Posts

This section is where the site becomes a blog rather than just a portfolio.

It should show:

- what you are building now
- what problems you are thinking about
- how you approach experiments
- what kind of mind you have

### About

This is where you earn depth.

It should explain:

- how you think
- why you build
- what kind of work excites you
- how software, automation, and hardware connect in your world

This should feel personal, but still useful. Not oversharing. Not a life story. Just enough depth that people understand the person behind the work.

### Connect / Elsewhere

This section should be simple:

- how to contact you
- where to follow your work
- where to see more of what you build
- where to get your CV if needed

## What To Put In Each Sanity Document Type

This section maps directly to your current Studio structure.

### Home

Your `Home` singleton should carry the top-level story.

Use it for:

- your current headline
- current focus
- homepage featured project cards
- homepage build-log cards
- your broader mission
- the more personal “operating manual” content
- contact and elsewhere links

This is where you shape perception.

### Project

A `Project` entry should be a case study, not just a gallery card.

Each project should include:

- **Title**
  Clear and memorable.
- **Excerpt**
  One-sentence summary for listings.
- **Description**
  Slightly longer summary that explains why it matters.
- **Main image**
  A real screenshot, interface, device, or visual reference.
- **Body**
  The actual story of the project.
- **Tags**
  Only tags that genuinely help explain the project.

Each project body should answer:

- What problem was this solving?
- Who was it for?
- What was your role?
- What did you build?
- What constraints mattered?
- What was technically interesting?
- What happened after launch?
- What would you improve next?

The best projects for your site are not necessarily the most polished. They are the ones that best demonstrate:

- speed
- ownership
- practical problem solving
- unusual range
- good judgment

### Experience

An `Experience` entry should explain the role, not just record it.

For each role:

- title and company should be clean and factual
- the body should describe your real contribution
- include systems built, responsibilities held, or the kind of environment you worked in

Good experience entries talk about:

- production systems
- legacy modernization
- internal tools
- cross-functional work
- automation and reporting
- product ownership

Avoid writing them like LinkedIn bullet spam.

### Post

Your `Post` content is what makes the site feel alive.

Posts should show:

- how you think
- how you build
- what you notice
- what you are testing

Your current post categories already support a strong editorial mix:

- `build`
- `dev-log`
- `brain-dump`
- `tutorial`
- `idea`
- `automation`
- `stack`
- `hardware`
- `meta`

That is enough. Do not overcomplicate the taxonomy.

## What Kinds Of Posts You Should Write

Your blog should sit between:

- build journal
- technical notebook
- product thinking
- experimental founder log

It should feel like reading notes from someone who is actively making things.

### Post Type 1: Build Logs

These are the strongest fit for you.

Examples:

- how you built a feature
- how you approached a product experiment
- what happened when a build failed
- the first version of a system and how it changed

This content makes you look active, real, and credible.

### Post Type 2: Project Breakdowns

Write posts that expand a project beyond its case-study page.

Examples:

- why you built it
- what assumptions were wrong
- what users actually cared about
- what tradeoffs you made

This gives your work depth.

### Post Type 3: Automation Notes

This is a strong differentiator for you.

Write about:

- AI agents in operations
- workflow design
- onboarding automation
- lead generation systems
- prompt and orchestration lessons
- what actually works vs what sounds impressive

### Post Type 4: Hardware / Edge Experiments

This content helps separate you from standard web portfolios.

Write about:

- Raspberry Pi builds
- redaction pipelines
- memory capture ideas
- always-on devices
- device constraints
- experiments that combine software and physical systems

### Post Type 5: Practical Opinions

Shorter posts about things you believe after building.

Examples:

- why MVP-first still matters
- why boring products are underrated
- what most people get wrong about automation
- why real testing beats speculation

### Post Type 6: Slightly Unhinged Side Notes

You should leave room for your weirder projects and ideas.

This matters because it makes the site feel like *you*.

Examples:

- ridiculous but real experiments
- weird local app ideas
- product concepts that probably should not exist but might work

Used carefully, this becomes personality rather than noise.

## Writing Style

Your writing should sound like:

- direct
- thoughtful
- practical
- slightly sharp
- grounded in real work

You do not need to sound polished in a corporate way.

You do need to sound:

- clear
- believable
- self-aware
- technically literate

Good tone:

- “This worked.”
- “This part was wrong.”
- “I changed my mind after testing it.”
- “This is useful, even if it is not glamorous.”

Avoid:

- fake authority
- LinkedIn-inspirational tone
- excessive hype
- trying to sound like a VC-backed startup page

Your edge is not just coding ability.

Your edge is the combination of:

- product thinking
- speed
- automation thinking
- hardware curiosity
- founder energy
- practical execution

Your content should keep returning to that combination.

If the site becomes just:

- stacks

## What To Avoid

- too many empty claims about innovation
- generic skill lists with no proof
- over-explaining basic technology
- trying to sound bigger than you are
- turning the site into a pure résumé
- turning the blog into generic tutorials no one needed from you

The content should stay close to lived experience.

## Publishing Cadence

A sustainable rhythm is:

- update homepage featured cards when your priorities change
- publish 1 post every 2 to 4 weeks
- add or improve 1 project case study per month when possible
- keep the work section factual and current

This is enough to make the site feel active without becoming maintenance-heavy.

## Simple Content Checklist

Before publishing anything on the site, ask:

- Does this show something real?
- Does this sound like me?
- Does this add proof, insight, or personality?
- Does this help someone understand how I build?
- Is this more useful than generic?

If the answer is no, it probably does not belong.
